\section{Problem 3: Speech Recognition from Speech Spectrum with Augmentation}

This problem targets spoken-digit recognition (digits 0--9) using spectrogram images as
the primary representation.  Four experimental conditions are studied:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\alph*)]
    \item Baseline CNN trained on original spectrograms (no augmentation).
    \item Re-training after \emph{speech-domain} augmentation (time-stretching, additive noise).
    \item Re-training after \emph{image-domain} augmentation (horizontal squeeze/expand, pixel noise).
    \item Re-training after \emph{combined} speech- and image-domain augmentation.
\end{enumerate}

% -----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Dataset and Preprocessing}

Each audio utterance was converted to a mel-spectrogram image using
\texttt{librosa.feature.melspectrogram} with the following settings:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Sampling rate: 22\,050\,Hz (resampled if necessary).
    \item FFT window: 2\,048 samples; hop length: 512 samples.
    \item Mel filter banks: 128.
    \item Power spectrogram converted to dB scale via \texttt{librosa.power\_to\_db}.
    \item Final image resized to $128\times128$ pixels (single-channel greyscale).
\end{itemize}
Images were normalised to $[0,1]$ by dividing by 255.  Labels were taken from the
folder names (one sub-folder per digit class).

\subsection{Speech-Domain Augmentation (Part b)}

To improve robustness to speaking-rate and acoustic-environment variability, two
speech-level transformations were applied \emph{before} spectrogram extraction:

\begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Time-stretching.}  Each waveform was sped up by 3\,\% and slowed down by
            3\,\% using \texttt{librosa.effects.time\_stretch} with rates $r=1.03$ and
          $r=0.97$, effectively tripling the dataset size (original + fast + slow).
    \item \textbf{Additive waveform noise.}  Zero-mean Gaussian noise with
          $\sigma = 0.005\times\max(|x|)$ was added to each original waveform,
          yielding one additional noisy copy per utterance.
\end{itemize}

After augmentation the spectrograms were extracted using the same pipeline as Part~a.
The same CNN architecture and training protocol were used without modification.

\subsection{Image-Domain Augmentation (Part c)}

In this condition augmentation was applied directly to the spectrogram images,
\emph{after} conversion from audio, simulating temporal variability without re-processing
audio:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Horizontal squeeze (12\,\%).}  The image was resized to 88\,\% of its
        original width using bilinear interpolation and then zero-padded back to
        $128\times128$. This produces stronger temporal compression to simulate
        faster speech.
    \item \textbf{Horizontal expand (12\,\%).}  The image was resized to 112\,\% of its
        original width and then centre-cropped to $128\times128$, producing stronger
        temporal expansion to simulate slower speech.
    \item \textbf{Pixel noise.}  Zero-mean Gaussian noise with $\sigma=0.08$ (pixel
        values in $[0,1]$) was added and clipped back to $[0,1]$.
    \item \textbf{SpecAugment masks.}  Larger time/frequency masks were applied on the
        spectrogram tensor (SpecAugment with frequency/time widths $F=12$, $T=12$).
\end{itemize}

The original spectrogram together with the three augmented copies were used for training
(dataset multiplied by~4).  The CNN architecture and training protocol were identical
to Part~a.

\subsection{Combined Speech and Image Augmentation (Part d)}

Part~d combines all transformations from Parts~b and~c.  For every original waveform:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Three waveforms are produced: original, sped-up ($\times1.03$), slowed-down ($\times0.97$).
    \item Each waveform also receives one additional noisy copy (Gaussian waveform noise, $\sigma=0.005$).
    \item Spectrograms are extracted from all waveforms ($\times4$ waveforms per original utterance).
    \item Each spectrogram is then augmented with horizontal squeeze, horizontal expand, and pixel noise
          ($\times3$ additional image copies per spectrogram, 4 total including original).
\end{enumerate}

This yields up to $4\times4=16$ training examples per original utterance, providing
broad coverage of both temporal and spectral variability.

\subsection{Discussion}

\paragraph{Effect of speech-domain augmentation (Part b).}
Time-stretching creates training samples that differ temporally from the originals while
preserving spectral content, making the model more robust to speaking-rate variation.
Adding waveform noise mimics real recording conditions such as background interference
and microphone variability.  Because both transformations are applied \emph{before}
spectrogram extraction, the network sees genuinely different spectrograms rather than
pixel-level distortions, producing features that generalise better to unseen speakers.
The dataset size grows by a factor of four (original, speed-up, slow-down, noisy),
which helps reduce over-fitting on the relatively small spoken-digit corpus.

\paragraph{Effect of image-domain augmentation (Part c).}
Squeezing and expanding the spectrogram image along the time axis is computationally
cheaper than re-processing audio, yet achieves a similar effect: the CNN learns to be
invariant to small compressions or expansions of the time axis.  Pixel-level noise adds
robustness against quantisation artefacts and minor recording-equipment differences.
Because augmentation happens at the image level the audio pipeline runs only once, making
this approach attractive when audio preprocessing is a bottleneck.

\paragraph{Combined augmentation (Part d).}
Combining both strategies further diversifies the training set through a multiplicative
effect: speech augmentation followed by image augmentation can yield up to 16 training
examples per original utterance.  This typically yields the highest test accuracy but at
the cost of significantly longer training time.  If computational resources are limited,
image-domain augmentation alone (Part~c) is recommended as it achieves a favourable
accuracy-to-time trade-off without requiring audio re-processing.

\paragraph{Digit-level observations.}
From the confusion matrices (Figures~\ref{fig:p3a_cm}--\ref{fig:p3d_cm}) the most
frequently confused digit pairs tend to be acoustically similar, such as
\textit{one}/\textit{nine} or \textit{three}/\textit{five}, where discriminating cues
lie in brief formant transitions that can be masked by aggressive augmentation.  The
combined augmentation generally reduces these confusions by exposing the model to a
wider range of temporal patterns, at the cost of occasional new confusions introduced
by very strong distortions.
